Consumer Ada SM89: Measurements, Compiler Frontiers, and
Kernel Consequences
Anonymous Author(s)
Submission Id: #1738 – Confidential Draft – Do NOT Distribute!!
Abstract
We present an empirical characterization of consumer Ada SM89
covering 379 mnemonics under standard optimization, 382 under
the most permissive compilation configuration, and 25 measured
instruction latencies spanning scalar ALU, SFU, FP64, tensor-core,
memory, and synchronization paths. Dependent-chain microbench-
marks, disassembly, cubin-side patching, and differential testing
separate three commonly conflated questions: which instruction
forms compilers emit, which merely exist at the encoding level, and
which execute with semantically distinct behavior.
Two compiler gaps are characterized. No tested compiler path
(CUDA, PTX, clang, Triton, or ptxas sweeps) emits P2R.B1/B2/B3,
although cubin-side substitution proves all three forms valid and
executable on sm_89. Patching ULOP3 encodings to UPLOP3 yields
invalid cubins; patching from PLOP3 yields valid cubins whose 15
substitution sites partition into 12 that leave kernel output un-
changed and 3 that produce functionally distinct output. The mea-
sured TF32 WMMA path of 66.66 cycles is shown to correspond to a
decomposed 2x HMMA.1684.F32.TF32 path; the single-instruction
HMMA.1688.F32.TF32 form is separately confirmed by inline-PTX
disassembly.
Hand-tuned inline-PTX rewrites of three Instant-NGP kernels,
compared against reference CUDA implementations in the same bi-
nary under identical compiler flags, achieve 1.11×, 3.16×, and 1.53×
speedups for hash-grid encoding, MLP forward, and volume render-
ing, with exact or near-exact output preservation. In CUDA-LBM
at 1283, the measured MUFU.RCP latency of 41.55 cycles motivates
precomputing inv_tau to remove the per-cell reciprocal critical
path; the resulting tier map shows Q32.32 and FP64 at zero mass
drift, FP32 Fused at 4.77 × 10−7, and INT8 tiers at drift levels that
preclude conservation-sensitive use.

Measurement Basis and Scope
This work uses a mixed Ada evidence base. The broader SM89 cor-
pus was gathered historically on an RTX 4070 Ti Super; validation
reruns were then collected on a local RTX 4070 Ti over SSH. That
sequencing was driven by available hardware, not by a planned
cross-board comparison, so we treat the two datasets asymmetri-
cally: the Ti Super corpus provides breadth and coverage, while the
RTX 4070 Ti reruns anchor any measurement sensitive to board or
runtime configuration.
Concretely, the inventory counts come from the checked-in
broader corpus; the full 379-mnemonic frontier was not re-
enumerated on the RTX 4070 Ti rerun board. The TF32 tensor
decomposition result is confirmed by both corpora, while the
main CUDA-LBM application table and the representative LDG
pointer-chase row are anchored to fresh RTX 4070 Ti reruns at 1283.
The artifact still includes the larger set of custom CUDA probes,
dependent-chain and ILP microbenchmarks, disassembly pipelines,
processed CSV tables, cubin-side patch validation, and application
kernels. Each instruction family is characterized at three levels:
(1) Emission: does any compiler path produce the target SASS form
from source or IR?
(2) Validity: if no compiler emits it, does cubin-side substitution
produce a form that executes correctly on the same device?
(3) Semantics: does the patched form alter computed output under
differential testing, or leave output unchanged?
Scalar latency is measured using 512-deep dependent chains, a
single warp, one warmup pass, and 20 timed repetitions read from
the on-device clock (CS2R/S2UR); disassembly of each probe cu-
bin is preserved alongside each result. Run-to-run variance across
the 20 repetitions is below measurement resolution for all scalar
latencies reported here; the cross-board agreement in Table 3 (com-
pute metrics within 0.01–0.02 cycles across two different boards)
bounds the total measurement noise empirically. Tensor latency
uses 256-deep WMMA-dependent chains, since tensor API calls
expand to multiple SASS instructions. Throughput measurements
exclude –use_fast_math paths, which alter instruction selection

Related Work
Instruction-level microbenchmarking has become the standard way
to recover the parts of NVIDIA GPUs that vendor manuals leave
implicit. Jia et al. established that style on Volta and then Turing,
measuring instruction latency, throughput, memory hierarchy be-
havior, and selected tensor-core properties [2, 3]. Abdelkhalik et
al. extended the same basic approach to Ampere and emphasized
instruction timing, memory access cost, and tensor-core behavior
on a newer ISA surface [1]. Luo et al. then moved the discussion to
Hopper, comparing Hopper against Ada and Ampere while empha-
sizing the new features unique to Hopper [4].
Our work differs from that line in three ways. First, we focus
on a consumer Ada target rather than a datacenter board. Second,
we treat compiler frontiers as first-class results: we are explicit
about what direct source or IR still does not emit, what cubin-side
patching proves exists, and where runtime semantics are already
classified rather than merely guessed. Third, we require applica-
tion consequences. The Instant-NGP and CUDA-LBM sections are
therefore not decorative case studies; they are the evidence that
the measured machine facts actually change how kernels should
be written.

5
Compiler and Codegen Frontiers
We use frontier to mean an instruction form where opcode existence
and compiler emission diverge: the encoding is reachable via cubin
patching but no tested source or IR path selects it. Opcode discovery
on SM89 is mostly complete; two such frontiers remain and are the
subject of this section.
5.1
P2R.B1/B2/B3: selection versus existence
No compiler path we tested emits P2R.B1/B2/B3 directly. Exten-
sive source mutation, PTX search, frontend variation, and ptxas
sweeps reproduce the surrounding P2R neighborhood (including
plain P2R 0x7f and 0x0f), but the byte-qualified forms are never
selected. Cubin-side substitution proves all three byte forms valid
and runnable on the same machine. The open question is there-
fore not whether these opcodes exist; it is which backend selection
rule prevents them from being emitted. We do not claim to have
explained that mechanism. It could reflect backend conservatism,
a correctness hazard, cost-modeling, or historical implementation
debt; isolating which of those is responsible is future work.
5.2
UPLOP3.LUT: from structural validity to
runtime semantics
Patching ULOP3 encodings to UPLOP3 produces a cubin that
cuobjdump rejects as invalid. Patching from PLOP3 instead pro-
duces a cubin that disassembles and executes correctly on sm_89. Of
the 15 substitution sites tested, 12 leave kernel output unchanged;
3 produce different computed results. The sites uniform_occ1 and
cutlass_occ5 show the strongest and most consistent output
changes; uniform_occ2, cutlass_occ4, and uniform_occ5 alter
output only in certain operand combinations.
The methodological lesson is that source mutation, frontend
variation, and ptxas sweeps are best used to bound negative space;
cubin patching resolves existence questions; and differential fuzzing
is the most powerful semantic discriminator once a patched form
executes safely.

Application Case Studies
6.1
Instant-NGP hot loops
The strongest argument for the measurement corpus is that it
changes real kernels. The Instant-NGP hot-loop suite of Muller
et al. [5] is implemented here in inline PTX rather than raw SASS
because it still compiles with standard toolchains while giving
instruction-level control. Speedups are reported against reference
CUDA implementations of the same kernels compiled in the same
binary with identical flags (nvcc -arch=sm_89 -O2); both reference
and hand-tuned versions pass element-wise validation against the
same input set. The wins map directly onto the measured machine.
Hash-grid encoding is memory bound, so its final 1.11× speedup
comes from vectorized loads and software pipelining rather than
brute-force ALU changes. That final win was not immediate: the
first hand-written version was slower than the compiler at 0.69×,
an intermediate cleanup reached only 1.03×, and only the third
version reached 1.11×. MLP forward is compute bound, so it bene-
fits from deep FFMA ILP and shared memory tiling, yielding 3.16×.
Volume rendering sits in between and benefits from fast MUFU.EX2

near-exact outputs while exposing optimization levers that the
standard CUDA pipeline does not reliably reach on its own.
6.2
CUDA-LBM suite
The CUDA-LBM suite shows the same bridge on a larger design
surface. During revision, we reran the main 1283 production tiers
on the local RTX 4070 Ti over SSH/tmux so that the primary appli-
cation table is anchored to one board. The performance/accuracy
frontier depends on layout, storage precision, and whether the
collision path still pays the MUFU.RCP-dominated 1 / tau critical
path. The most important kernel-level optimization is precomput-
ing inv_tau. The measured MUFU.RCP latency of 41.55 cycles, plus
the additional iterative reciprocal cost discussed in the checked-in
LBM notes, makes per-cell reciprocal work too expensive to ignore.
Precomputing inv_tau removes that critical path from every time
step and materially changes which tiers are attractive.
Table 9 highlights three nontrivial consequences. First, raw
throughput and physics quality diverge sharply at low precision.
INT8 SoA is the fastest tier here, but a mass drift of 3.12e−01 makes
it physically nonviable for conservation-sensitive use; it is best
treated as a performance probe, not as a production physics tier.
INT8 SoA Lloyd-Max materially reduces drift, but at 1.47e−02 it
is still an approximate low-precision tier rather than a zero-drift
one. Second, layout dominates vectorization: the reruns again
show that SoA is where the large gains come from, and the local
INT8 SoA / INT8 SoA LM / Q32.32 ordering is unambiguous. Third,
Q32.32 matters because it bypasses consumer Ada’s weak FP64
path while preserving zero observed drift in the shipped validation
suite. The historical RTX 4070 Ti Super dataset preserves the
same qualitative ordering, but because the two boards were not
benchmarked as a synchronized head-to-head study, we do not
interpret the absolute Ti-vs-Ti-Super deltas. That is exactly the
kind of architecture-to-kernel bridge a SASS study should deliver.
This is also where the bounded approach helps, but it should
not be used to flatten the design space into five equally acceptable
points. What the data support is narrower and more useful: a long
reciprocal path pushes the collision kernel toward precomputed
inv_tau; consumer Ada’s weak FP64 rate makes fixed-point viable
again; Q32.32 and FP64 are the zero-drift reference tiers; FP32 Fused
is the practical low-drift baseline; and the INT8 tiers buy throughput
only by accepting physically lossy behavior.
7
Discussion
Three higher-level lessons emerge from the combined evidence.
First, knowing that an instruction exists is not the same as knowing
whether a compiler will emit it. We distinguish these cleanly: some
instruction families can be produced by cubin patching even though
no compiler path selects them, and others are already characterized
at the level of runtime semantics.
Second, mixed-hardware evidence is acceptable only when han-
dled asymmetrically. The cross-board sanity checks suggest that
short dependent-chain compute measurements are comparatively
stable across the RTX 4070 Ti and RTX 4070 Ti Super, while memory-
path timing is more configuration-sensitive. That justifies using
local reruns to anchor scrutiny-sensitive claims, admitting explicitly
that the full inventory was historically enumerated on the Ti Super
corpus, and treating the broader checked-in corpus as supporting
coverage rather than as a pseudo-controlled comparison.
Third, the application case studies prevent the work from col-
lapsing into cataloging. Instant-NGP and CUDA-LBM are included
because they make the measurements earn their place. Remove the
measurements and the rewrites lose their explanation; remove the
applications and the measurements lose much of their architectural
value.
8
Threats to Validity and Scope
The claims are deliberately bounded in four ways.
First, neither P2R.B* nor UPLOP3.LUT can yet be emitted directly
from source or IR. For P2R, the mechanism behind compiler non-
selection also remains unexplained.
Second, we separate broader checked-in corpus evidence from
rerun-backed primary tables. The inventory counts come from the
historically collected Ada corpus, while the tensor wording repair,
the representative LDG pointer-chase row, and the main CUDA-
LBM application table are anchored to revision-time RTX 4070 Ti
reruns inside a remote tmux session. Those two sources agree on
the tensor-form interpretation, but they were not collected as a
normalized cross-board study. We therefore treat the RTX 4070

9
Conclusion
Consumer Ada SM89 supports a stable inventory of 379 canoni-
cal mnemonics, two bounded compiler frontiers, a corrected ten-
sor decomposition story that separates WMMA paths from direct
single-instruction forms, and application consequences that turn
those measurements into actionable kernel decisions. The remain-
ing open questions (the selection rule behind P2R.B* non-emission
and the full semantic scope of live UPLOP3 substitutions) are scoped
explicitly. Together, these results show that instruction-level mi-
crobenchmarking on consumer Ada produces bounded, mechanistic
claims rather than isolated latency tables.
